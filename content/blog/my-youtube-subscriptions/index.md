YouTube is the strangest bookshelf I own.

It has lectures from people who build programming languages, live streams from open source maintainers, guitar arrangements I will probably never play cleanly, sketching channels that make me want to draw again, and a handful of videos that exist purely because the internet is allowed to be unserious.

I recently looked through my subscriptions and found **161 channels**. That number surprised me less than the shape of the list. My YouTube is not random. It is a map of what I keep returning to when I want to learn, practice, relax, or feel pulled back toward making things.

## The quick stats

These numbers came from my YouTube subscriptions page on **9 July 2026**. YouTube showed subscriber counts for 157 of the 161 channels, so subscriber-based stats below use only the visible counts.

| Genre | Channels |
| --- | ---: |
| Technology, programming, and software craft | 104 |
| Art, sketching, and visual taste | 17 |
| Music, guitar, and performance | 16 |
| Playful internet, sports, and gaming | 15 |
| Essays, podcasts, and reflective culture | 9 |

A few details stood out:

- **64% of my subscriptions are technical.** This is very on-brand.
- **70 channels have fewer than 10,000 subscribers.** I apparently like small rooms where people are still figuring things out in public.
- **26 channels have fewer than 1,000 subscribers.** Some of the best YouTube is still extremely handmade.
- The visible combined subscriber count across my subscriptions is about **51.3 million**. That is not my audience, obviously. It is just the public audience size of the channels I follow.
- The largest channel in my list is [Sungha Jung](https://www.youtube.com/@sunghajung), at about **7.21 million subscribers**.
- The largest technical channel in my list is [Andrej Karpathy](https://www.youtube.com/@AndrejKarpathy), at about **1.55 million subscribers**.

## Technology, programming, and software craft

But the pattern I like more is not fame. It is proximity to the work. I subscribe to people who build things in front of me: editors, shells, programming languages, databases, compilers, terminals, web frameworks, security tools, and weird experiments.

The common thread is craft. I like watching someone think through constraints. I like when a video is not just "here is the answer", but "here is how the answer emerges if we are patient enough with the system."

## Art, sketching, and visual taste

The second category is quieter, but important.

I follow [Super Ani](https://www.youtube.com/@superani), [Gawx Art](https://www.youtube.com/@GawxArt), [Chroma Moma](https://www.youtube.com/@ChromaMoma), [Josh Art](https://www.youtube.com/@JoshArt02), [Jillee Arts](https://www.youtube.com/@JilleeArts), [Valerie Lin](https://www.youtube.com/@valerielin), [Steven Zapata Art](https://www.youtube.com/@StevenZapataArt), [Kamulch_Pen Drawing](https://www.youtube.com/@Kamulch_Art), [The Wet Pen](https://www.youtube.com/@thewetpen), [pateseich](https://www.youtube.com/@pateseich), [marsketcher](https://www.youtube.com/@marsketcher), [arielbrindissketch](https://www.youtube.com/@arielbrindissketch), [Zephyer](https://www.youtube.com/@ZephyerArt), and a few more channels in that world.

I am not a professional illustrator. That is exactly why these channels matter.

Programming trains one kind of attention: correctness, structure, failure modes, abstractions, interfaces. Drawing trains another: proportion, patience, negative space, texture, gesture, light. It is good for my brain to spend time with people whose first instinct is not to name the thing, but to observe it.

These channels remind me that taste is practice. You do not get better taste only by consuming better work. You get it by noticing more.

I like guitar videos because they have no shortcut. You can fake confidence in software for a surprisingly long time. You cannot fake clean timing, tone, or muscle memory. The instrument tells on you immediately.

That makes music channels useful beyond music. They are evidence of repetition. They make practice visible.

## Why I pay for YouTube Premium

My account currently shows an **Individual YouTube Premium membership at ₹149/month**. It also shows a Family plan offer at **₹299/month**.

I pay because YouTube is not just entertainment for me. It is part of my learning system.

When I watch a Karpathy lecture, a DjangoCon talk, a WezTerm update, a guitar performance, or a sketching process video, I am usually not doing it as passive background noise. I am trying to stay with a line of thought. Ads break that line.

Premium is easy to describe as "no ads", but that is not the real benefit. The real benefit is continuity:

- no interruption inside long technical talks,
- background play when I am walking or doing chores,
- downloads for travel or weak network days,
- fewer weird context switches when I am using YouTube as a study tool,
- and a cleaner boundary between "I chose this" and "the platform inserted this."

At ₹149/month, it is one of the few subscriptions where the value is obvious to me. Not because every minute on YouTube is noble. It is not. But because the minutes I do care about become much better.

## What this list says

My subscriptions are less a list of channels and more a set of recurring questions.

How do good programmers think? How do tools become pleasant? How do people practice for years without getting bored? How does taste compound? How do you make something small and still make it worth watching?

That is what I want YouTube to be for me: not a feed that eats the day, but a shelf I can return to when I want better inputs.

Premium helps because it makes that shelf quieter.
